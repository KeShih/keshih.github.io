Basis of the module Zn


A student who is taking linear algebra asked me the following problem.

If we consider the field R restricted to Z, and create a “vector space” on Z. How do we know if v,u∈Z2 “spans” Z2?

Formally, what can we say about v and u if for every w∈Z2, there exist n,m∈Z, such that nv+mu=w.

We can generalize it and put it in terms of modules, as Z is only a ring but not a field.

Theorem1

v1​,…,vn​ is a basis for the module Zn iff the matrix M formed by the vectors is a unimodular matrix.

Proof

⇒ If det(M)=0, then v1​,…,vn​ are not linearly independent. If ∣det(M)∣≥2, then the parallelepiped formed by v1​,…,vn​ has volume ≥2. If there is any integer point not on the corners of the parallelepiped, then that point can’t be written as linear combination of v1​,…,vn​. Notice that it must contain some lattice points not on the corners of the parallelepiped. One can see why by consider a large box that contain volume of m such parallelepiped, but contain at least 2m lattice points.

This shows if M is not unimodular, then v1​,…,vn​ can’t be a basis.

Alternative proof: M is not unimodular then M−1 contain a non-integer entry. This shows there exist a b, such that the solution x to Mx=b contain a non-integer entry. (proposed by Thao Do)

⇐ ∣det(M)∣=1 implies it has a inverse over Z, thus Mx=b for any b∈Zn always has a solution.
