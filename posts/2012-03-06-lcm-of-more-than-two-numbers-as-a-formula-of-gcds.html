lcm⁡\lcmlcm of more than two numbers as a formula of gcd⁡\gcdgcds


It is a common elementary number theory exercise to prove that lcm⁡(a,b)=abgcd⁡(a,b)\lcm(a,b) = \frac{ab}{\gcd(a,b)}lcm(a,b)=gcd(a,b)ab​.

A student might ask what is the lcm⁡\lcmlcm of three numbers. Some might think that lcm⁡(a,b,c)=abcgcd⁡(a,b,c) \lcm(a,b,c) = \frac{abc}{\gcd(a,b,c)} lcm(a,b,c)=gcd(a,b,c)abc​ It isn’t.

Still, one might want a formula for the lcm⁡\lcmlcm of three numbers. Of course one can say lcm⁡(a,lcm⁡(b,c))\lcm(a,\lcm(b,c))lcm(a,lcm(b,c)). In fact this is the common algorithm for computation. Are they ways to relate lcm⁡\lcmlcm and gcd⁡\gcdgcd without nesting those functions together?

Yes, but the formula is not so pretty. lcm⁡(a,b,c)=abcgcd⁡(a,b,c)gcd⁡(a,b)gcd⁡(b,c)gcd⁡(a,c) \lcm(a,b,c) = \frac{abc \gcd(a,b,c)}{\gcd(a,b)\gcd(b,c)\gcd(a,c)} lcm(a,b,c)=gcd(a,b)gcd(b,c)gcd(a,c)abcgcd(a,b,c)​

This article shows how we can prove this result, and easily infer a more general theorem. It’s easy to show lcm⁡(a1,a2,…,an)=f−1(max⁡(f(a1),…,f(an)))gcd⁡(a1,a2,…,an)=f−1(min⁡(f(a1),…,f(an)))\begin{aligned} \lcm(a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_n) &= f^{-1} (\max(f(a_1),\ldots,f(a_n)))\\ \gcd(a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_n) &= f^{-1} (\min(f(a_1),\ldots,f(a_n))) \end{aligned}lcm(a1​,a2​,…,an​)gcd(a1​,a2​,…,an​)​=f−1(max(f(a1​),…,f(an​)))=f−1(min(f(a1​),…,f(an​)))​ where max⁡\maxmax and min⁡\minmin are defined coordinate-wise. In fact we only need to concern with one single coordinate. So the problem become proving max⁡(a,b,c)=a+b+c+min⁡(a,b,c)−(min⁡(a,b)+min⁡(b,c)+min⁡(a,c)) \max(a,b,c) = a+b+c+\min(a,b,c)-(\min(a,b)+\min(b,c)+\min(a,c)) max(a,b,c)=a+b+c+min(a,b,c)−(min(a,b)+min(b,c)+min(a,c)), then the formula for lcm⁡\lcmlcm of 3 numbers holds.

This look familiar to the inclusion-exclusion principle, and certainly we can use it to prove it and generalize! Let μ\muμ be the Lebesgue measure, then for a finite sequence of non-negative reals {ai}\set{a_i}{ai​}, max⁡(a1,…,an)=μ(⋃i=1n[0,ai]).\max(a_1,\ldots,a_n) = \mu(\bigcup_{i=1}^n [0,a_i]).max(a1​,…,an​)=μ(i=1⋃n​[0,ai​]). It’s just some standard arguments to show max⁡\maxmax does have the inclusion-exclusion structure. It generalize to allow negative reals by simply add a large enough constant to make them positive, and subtract the constant from the result.
